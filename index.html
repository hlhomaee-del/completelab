Revenue Cycle & Claim Lifecycle

From patient registration to payment — every stage, every document, every trap.

What is the Revenue Cycle?

The revenue cycle is the entire financial journey of a patient encounter — from the moment a patient calls to schedule an appointment all the way to when the provider receives final payment (or writes off a balance).

🔄 Revenue Cycle — The 10 Key Stages

1. Scheduling & Pre-Registration
Collect demographic + insurance info before the visit
2. Eligibility Verification
Confirm active coverage and benefits before service
3. Charge Capture
Record all services rendered using codes
4. Claim Preparation (Coding)
Convert documented services into billable claim
5. Claim Submission
Send claim to payer (electronic or paper)
6. Claim Adjudication
Payer reviews, processes, and determines payment
7. Payment Posting
Record payment/EOB in practice management system
8. Denial Management
Identify, appeal, and resubmit denied/rejected claims
9. Patient Billing
Bill patient for remaining balance (copay, coinsurance, deductible)
10. Accounts Receivable Follow-Up
Track and collect outstanding balances; prevent delinquency
⚠️ Test Trap: The CBCS exam may ask which stage comes FIRST or LAST. Eligibility verification always happens BEFORE services — not after. Discharge triggers the billing stage, not registration.
🏥 Workplace Skill: A billing specialist must understand where a claim is in the cycle to know who to contact, what to fix, and what comes next.

Claim Lifecycle

A claim doesn't just get submitted and paid. It goes through a lifecycle — and every stage has a specific name, a specific action, and specific consequences if mishandled.

📤 Charge Capture

The process of recording all services, procedures, and supplies provided to a patient. Charge capture translates clinical documentation into billable codes so a claim can be created.

  • Services not documented = can't be billed
  • Missing codes = claim underpaid or denied
  • Incorrect codes = fraud risk or rejection

📋 EHR + Requisition

The EHR provides the clinical record. A requisition is a formal order (like a lab order). Together, they support charge capture and claims documentation.

If asked what supports charge capture — the answer combines EHR documentation + the requisition/order. The EHR alone doesn't generate the charge.

⚡ Claim Submission Sequence

The order in which a claim must be submitted to payers matters for coordination of benefits (COB):

Step 1: Submit to primary payer first
Step 2: Receive primary EOB/remittance
Step 3: Submit to secondary payer with primary's EOB attached
Step 4: Receive secondary payment (if applicable)
Step 5: Bill patient for remaining balance
⚠️Trap: You cannot submit to the secondary payer UNTIL the primary has paid or denied. Submitting both simultaneously is wrong on the exam.
📝 A patient has both Medicare and a private insurance plan. The biller submits the claim to both payers at the same time. What is the problem with this approach?

🏥 The Discharge Stage in the Revenue Cycle

The discharge stage is the trigger point in the inpatient revenue cycle that starts the billing process. When a patient is discharged, clinical documentation is finalized, codes are assigned, and the claim is built.

✅ What happens at discharge?

  • Attending physician documents discharge summary
  • All diagnoses and procedures are finalized
  • HIM / coding team assigns ICD-10 + CPT/HCPCS
  • Charge capture is completed
  • Claim is prepared and submitted

⚠️ Why it matters for billing

  • Billing cannot begin until discharge is complete
  • Incomplete documentation = delayed claim = delayed revenue
  • Discharge triggers the timely filing clock
  • Queries to physicians may be needed before coding can start
⚠️ Test Trap: Students confuse the registration stage (when billing begins gathering info) with the discharge stage (when billing ACTUALLY starts). On the exam, discharge = billing trigger for inpatient.
📝 In the inpatient revenue cycle, which event officially triggers the billing process?

Insurance, COB & Payer Logic

Assignment of benefits · COB · Birthday Rule · Coinsurance · MSN · Eligibility

📋 Assignment of Benefits

Assignment of benefits (AOB) is the patient's written authorization directing the insurance company to pay the provider directly — instead of sending the reimbursement check to the patient.

✅ WITH Assignment of Benefits

  • Payer sends payment directly to provider
  • Patient only owes their cost-share (copay/coinsurance)
  • Streamlines collections for the practice
  • Required for Medicare participating providers

❌ WITHOUT Assignment of Benefits

  • Payer sends check to the patient
  • Provider must collect full amount from patient
  • Risk of patient not forwarding payment
  • Cash flow problems for the practice
⚠️ Test Trap: AOB does NOT mean the patient is waiving their cost-share. It only changes WHO the insurance company sends the money TO. The patient still owes copays and coinsurance.
🏥 Workplace Skill: Front desk staff must ensure patients sign the AOB form at every new encounter. Without it, checks go to patients and revenue is at risk.
📝 A patient signs an assignment of benefits form. What is the DIRECT result of this action?

⚖️ Coordination of Benefits (COB)

COB prevents double-payment when a patient has more than one insurance plan. It establishes a specific order: primary payer pays first, secondary payer pays the remainder (up to plan limits).

🎂 The Birthday Rule

When a dependent child is covered under BOTH parents' insurance plans, the birthday rule determines which parent's plan is primary:


🎂 The parent whose birthday falls EARLIEST in the calendar year (month and day — NOT year) has the primary plan for the child.


⚠️ Major Trap: Birthday rule is based on the MONTH and DAY only — NOT the year of birth. A parent born January 15 in 1990 is primary over a parent born February 3 in 1975.
🔍 Knowledge Gap: The birthday rule only applies to DEPENDENT CHILDREN. It does not apply to determine COB for the parents themselves.

💰 Coinsurance, Copay & Allowed Amount

These three concepts are closely related — and the CBCS exam will test your ability to calculate and distinguish them.

TermDefinitionExample
Allowed Amount The maximum amount a payer will pay for a service (also called "contractual rate" or "negotiated rate") Provider bills $500; payer's allowed amount = $300
Coinsurance Patient's percentage share of the allowed amount AFTER the deductible is met Patient owes 20% of $300 = $60
Copayment (Copay) A fixed dollar amount the patient pays at time of service, regardless of the allowed amount Patient pays $25 copay at every visit
Deductible Amount patient must pay OUT OF POCKET before insurance begins to pay $1,000 annual deductible

🤝 Participating Provider (PAR)

A participating provider (PAR) has a signed contract with the insurance plan. By accepting PAR status, the provider agrees to:


  • Accept the plan's allowed amount as payment in full
  • Write off the difference between billed charges and allowed amount
  • NOT balance bill the patient beyond cost-share (copay/coinsurance)
  • Submit claims on behalf of the patient
PAR ProviderNon-PAR Provider
ContractYes — contracted with planNo — out-of-network
Allowed amountAccepts it as payment in fullMay bill patient the difference
Write-offRequired (contractual adjustment)Optional — can balance bill

⚠️ Test Trap: A PAR provider CANNOT balance bill a patient for the difference between their fee and the allowed amount — that's a contractual write-off, not patient responsibility. Non-PAR providers CAN balance bill.

📬 Medicare Summary Notice (MSN)

The Medicare Summary Notice (MSN) is sent by Medicare to the beneficiary (patient) — NOT to the provider. It summarizes all Medicare services billed during a specific period.

✅ What the MSN shows:

  • Services billed to Medicare on the patient's behalf
  • How much Medicare was billed
  • How much Medicare approved (allowed amount)
  • How much Medicare paid
  • Patient's responsibility (copay/coinsurance/deductible)

⚠️ What the MSN is NOT:

  • NOT a bill (patients do NOT pay from the MSN)
  • NOT an EOB (that goes to the provider)
  • NOT a claim form
  • NOT sent to the provider
⚠️ Test Trap: The MSN goes to the PATIENT. The Explanation of Benefits (EOB) / Remittance Advice goes to the PROVIDER. Students frequently mix these up.
🔍 Knowledge Gap: The MSN is specific to Medicare. Private insurers send an EOB to patients, not an MSN.

Documents, Files & Billing Tools

Chargemaster · Day Sheet · Electronic Flat File · Common Data File · Claims Attachment · EHR + Requisition

Why Billing Tools Matter

Every billing document and file has a specific job. Using the wrong tool — or not knowing which document supports which function — leads to claim errors, delays, and denials. The CBCS exam tests whether you know the purpose and use context of each tool, not just its name.

⚠️ Top Test Trap: The chargemaster and the day sheet are NOT the same. The chargemaster = permanent master pricing list. The day sheet = daily running log of transactions. One is a reference; one is a record.

The Charge Description Master (CDM) — also called the chargemaster — is a comprehensive, facility-maintained database that contains the standard charge for every service, procedure, supply, and medication a hospital provides. Think of it as the hospital's price list.

Each entry typically contains:

  • Service/procedure description
  • Charge code (facility-specific)
  • CPT/HCPCS code
  • Revenue code
  • Standard charge amount
🏥Workplace: When a patient has a procedure, the EHR triggers the chargemaster to pull the correct charge code. Without an accurate CDM, charges are wrong from the start.
🔍Knowledge Gap: The chargemaster does NOT list payer-specific allowed amounts — it lists the facility's full standard charge.

A day sheet (also called a daily journal) is a chronological record of all financial transactions in a medical practice for a single day. It captures every charge entered, every payment received, and every adjustment made.

Day sheet entries include:

  • Patient charges posted that day
  • Insurance payments received
  • Patient payments (copays, balances)
  • Adjustments and write-offs
  • Beginning and ending balances
🏥Workplace: The day sheet is reconciled at end of day to verify all transactions balance. It is used for daily financial reporting and auditing.
⚠️Trap: Day sheet = DAILY TRANSACTIONS. Chargemaster = MASTER PRICE LIST. One tracks activity; one sets pricing.

An electronic flat file is a standardized data file format used to transmit claim information electronically. The most common formats in healthcare billing are the 837P (professional claims) and 837I (institutional/hospital claims), as mandated by HIPAA.

💡A flat file is called "flat" because all data is stored in a plain text format with defined fields — there are no tables, no database relationships, just structured rows of data.
🏥Workplace: When a biller submits claims electronically, the practice management system generates an 837 flat file that the clearinghouse translates before sending to the payer.
🔍Knowledge Gap: The electronic flat file IS the claim data — it is NOT the clinical documentation that supports the claim. Clinical support = claims attachment.

A claims attachment is supplemental clinical documentation submitted along with (or in response to a request about) a claim. It provides clinical evidence the payer needs to process or adjudicate the claim.

Common examples of claims attachments:

  • Lab or pathology reports
  • Operative notes
  • Medical records / clinical notes
  • X-ray or imaging reports
  • Referral letters or physician orders
⚠️Trap: A claims attachment is NOT the claim itself. It is the SUPPORTING documentation. Claims can be submitted without attachments, but payers may request them or require them for specific services.

A common data file is a data repository used in billing operations to store, organize, and track claim-related information across the revenue cycle. It allows large billing departments to manage and report on claim status, payer responses, and accounts receivable.

🔍Knowledge Gap: This is one of the least familiar concepts to students. It is NOT a claim form, NOT an EHR, and NOT an electronic flat file. It's a management/reporting data structure used behind the scenes in large billing operations.
🏥Workplace: In large health systems, the common data file connects billing activity across multiple departments and helps track claim outcomes and payer patterns.

Compliance & Legal Rules

HIPAA Covered Entity · Truth in Lending Act · Fair Credit Reporting Act

🔒 HIPAA Covered Entity

Not every organization that handles health information is required to comply with HIPAA. Only covered entities — and their business associates — must follow HIPAA's privacy and security rules.

✅ The Three Types of Covered Entities

🏥
Healthcare Providers

Who transmit PHI electronically (doctors, hospitals, labs, clinics)

🛡️
Health Plans

Insurance companies, HMOs, employer health plans, Medicare/Medicaid

🔄
Healthcare Clearinghouses

Entities that process nonstandard health information into standard formats

⚠️ Major Test Trap: NOT all businesses that see health data are covered entities. A marketing company that receives patient data from a hospital is a business associate — not a covered entity. The hospital is the covered entity.
🔍 Knowledge Gap: A provider is only a covered entity under HIPAA if they transmit health information electronically. A provider who uses only paper records and never transmits electronically is technically NOT a covered entity — though this is rare today.

📃 Truth in Lending Act (TILA)

The Truth in Lending Act (TILA) is a federal consumer protection law that requires lenders — including healthcare providers offering payment plans with finance charges — to fully disclose the terms of credit before the agreement is signed.

✅ TILA Requires Disclosure Of:

  • Annual Percentage Rate (APR)
  • Total amount financed
  • Total number of payments
  • Amount of each payment
  • Total amount to be repaid
  • Any finance charges or fees

⚠️ When TILA Applies in Healthcare:

  • Patient signs a payment plan with interest or finance charge
  • Practice offers in-house financing for a balance
  • Third-party financing is arranged for services

⚠️Trap: If a patient pays in equal installments with NO finance charge, TILA does NOT apply. TILA is triggered by the presence of a finance charge.
🔍 Knowledge Gap: Students often apply TILA to insurance payment scenarios. TILA only applies to PATIENT credit/payment plan arrangements — not to insurance reimbursement.
🏥 Workplace Skill: Before a patient signs any payment plan that includes interest or fees, the billing office must provide a TILA disclosure. Failure to do so is a federal violation.
📝 A patient cannot pay their $1,200 balance in full. The practice sets up a 12-month payment plan with a 6% annual finance charge. Which law requires the practice to disclose the terms of this arrangement BEFORE the patient signs?

📊 Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) protects patients' rights when their credit information is used, checked, or reported to credit agencies. It governs how medical debt is reported and how patients can challenge inaccurate information.

✅ FCRA Gives Patients the Right To:

  • Know when their credit report is used against them
  • Dispute inaccurate credit information
  • Have errors investigated and corrected
  • Know what is in their credit file
  • Limit who can access their credit report

⚠️ When FCRA Applies in Healthcare:

  • Medical account is sent to a collection agency
  • Medical debt is reported to a credit bureau
  • Credit check is run on a patient
  • Patient disputes a medical account on their credit report
⚠️ Test Trap: FCRA ≠ HIPAA. HIPAA protects health information privacy. FCRA protects credit reporting rights. Both may apply when a delinquent account goes to collections — but they protect different things.
🔍 Knowledge Gap: Students confuse FCRA with TILA. TILA = terms of credit disclosed BEFORE signing. FCRA = rights regarding credit REPORTING after the fact.
📝 A patient discovers that a medical debt was incorrectly reported to a credit bureau. Which law gives the patient the right to dispute this inaccurate information?

Rejections, Denials & Follow-Up

Classify · Prioritize · Appeal · Prevent Delinquency

The Four Claim Status Types You Must Know

The CBCS exam will test your ability to tell these apart — and know the right action for each. They are NOT interchangeable.

REJECTED

Rejected Claim

The claim never entered the payer's system. It was returned due to a technical or formatting error before processing could begin.

⚠️Action: Fix the error. Resubmit as a NEW claim. You cannot appeal a rejected claim.

Examples: missing NPI, invalid member ID, missing diagnosis code

DENIED

Denied Claim

The claim entered the payer's system and was processed, but the payer determined it should not be paid.

⚠️Action: Review denial reason. Correct + appeal OR resubmit with documentation.

Examples: not medically necessary, no prior auth, not covered, COB issue

SUSPENDED

Suspended Claim

The claim is in the payer's system but has been placed on hold pending additional information or review before a payment decision is made.

🔍Action: Respond to payer's request for information promptly.

Examples: payer needs records, COB verification pending, concurrent review ongoing

DELINQUENT

Delinquent Claim

The claim was submitted but has received no response within the expected timeframe. This is an accounts receivable follow-up issue.

🟣Action: Contact payer to obtain claim status. Resubmit if no record found.

Example: 45+ days with no response, no record in payer system

⚠️ The Big Trap: Students confuse rejected and denied constantly. The key difference is whether the claim entered the payer's system. If it did = denied or suspended. If it didn't = rejected. The FOLLOW-UP ACTION is different for each.

Utilization Review Timing Lab

Preauthorization · Preadmission Review · Concurrent Review · Discharge Planning

What is Utilization Review?

Utilization review (UR) is the process insurance companies use to evaluate whether healthcare services are medically necessary, appropriate, and delivered in the most cost-effective setting. UR protects both the patient (against unnecessary procedures) and the payer (against unnecessary costs).

✅ Purpose of UR

  • Ensure services are medically necessary
  • Verify the appropriate level of care
  • Prevent unnecessary hospitalizations
  • Coordinate transitions of care
  • Control healthcare costs for the payer
  • Support patient safety and quality

⚠️ Why UR Matters for Billing

  • No authorization = potential denial
  • Wrong UR type cited = claim error
  • Missing concurrent review = inpatient days denied
  • Failed discharge planning = readmission penalties
  • Timing of UR type affects claim validity
⚠️ Master Test Trap: All four UR types sound similar. The ONLY reliable way to tell them apart is TIMING — when in the patient's care journey does each one occur? Learn the timing clues cold.

The 4 Utilization Review Types

Each type occurs at a different point in the patient care journey. Knowing WHEN is the key to never mixing them up.

BEFORE SERVICE

🔵 Preauthorization

Approval obtained from the payer before a non-emergency, elective service or procedure is performed. Applies broadly to outpatient procedures, specialist referrals, imaging, and planned surgeries.

⏰ Timing: Before any scheduled non-emergency service
🔑 Clue words: "prior to," "scheduled," "elective," "outpatient referral"
BEFORE ADMISSION

🟣 Preadmission Review

A UR review conducted specifically before a planned inpatient hospital admission. The insurance company reviews whether the admission is medically necessary before the patient is admitted.

⏰ Timing: Before a planned INPATIENT admission
🔑 Clue words: "planned admission," "inpatient," "before hospitalization"
DURING STAY

🟢 Concurrent Review

UR review conducted during an active inpatient stay to assess whether continued hospitalization is medically necessary. The payer evaluates ongoing care in real time.

⏰ Timing: While the patient is currently admitted (DURING)
🔑 Clue words: "currently admitted," "ongoing stay," "continued necessity"
AT/AFTER DISCHARGE

🟩 Discharge Planning

UR activity that coordinates post-discharge care and placement to ensure a safe transition from inpatient to the appropriate next level of care (home, rehab, skilled nursing facility).

⏰ Timing: At or near discharge — planning for what comes next
🔑 Clue words: "transitioning," "post-discharge," "placement," "next level of care"
🔍 Knowledge Gap: Preadmission review and preauthorization are NOT the same. Preauthorization applies broadly (outpatient services, referrals, etc.). Preadmission review is specifically for INPATIENT ADMISSIONS. A planned knee replacement surgery = preauthorization. Being admitted to the hospital the day before = preadmission review.
